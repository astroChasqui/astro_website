+ Siblings of the Sun

Stars are born in clusters of thousands to tens of thousands of stars, and our Sun was no exception. Where are the stars that were born together with the Sun now? These "siblings" of the Sun are difficult to find in the solar neighborhood. Nevertheless, a combination of kinematics and chemical abundance analysis has made it possible for us to find them. The star map below shows the location of HD162826, the first, and so far only, sibling of the Sun identified.

+ Exoplanet host stars

When planets form, they retain metals that would otherwise end up in the star. Thus, small metallicity deficiences are expected in planet-host stars. This effect is seen, for example, in the 16 Cygni binary system, where one would expect the component stars to have identical chemical composition. However, the secondary star, 16 Cyg B, is slightly metal-poor relative to the primary, 16 Cyg A.

16 Cyg B is known to host a gas giant planet. There have not yet been detections of planets around 16 Cyg A. We think that the metals that are missing in 16 Cyg B were used to make its planet. These chemical signatures of planet formation can be used to identify and/or investigate exoplanet populations.

We have detected similar signatures is the dual planet-host binary system XO-2. In this case we even detect metal differences that correlate with the elements' condensation temperature. It can be argued that these elemental abundance anomalies are due to the formation of Earth-like planets.

Related paper: The Dissimilar Chemical Composition of the Planet-hosting Stars of the XO-2 Binary System.

+ Galactic chemical evolution

The solar neighborhood contains stars from the so-called thin-disk (black crosses) as well as stars from the thick-disk (red circles) of the Milky Way. Their oxygen-to-iron abundance ratios are dependent on their kinematics, but the correlation is not perfect.

This suggests that the Galactic thin and thick disks are not independent entities, but different extremes of the overall disk population. In addition to mergers with small satelite galaxies, radial mixing (stars traveling large distances towards and away from the Galactic center) may be required to explain these data.

Paper: Oxygen Abundances in Nearby FGK Stars and the Galactic Chemical Evolution of the Local Disk and Halo.

+ Surface convection

The surfaces of cool stars are not static. Hot ascending cells of gas are surrounded by cool regions that fall back into the star's interior. This phenomenon, granulation, is observed in images of the Sun's surface. For distant stars, like the very-metal poor red giant star HD122563 shown below, the effects of surface convection can be seen in very high-quality spectra, for example by measuring the shape and amplitude of spectral line bisectors.

These observations help theorists calculate 3D hydrodynamic models of stellar atmospheres. The dashed line is where all the line bisectors should be if standard static, one-dimensional models of stellar atmospheres, which some astronomers use on a daily basis, were correct.

Paper: Granulation Signatures in the Spectrum of the Very Metal-poor Red Giant HD 122563.

+ Lithium depletion

Lithium is a fragile element that allows us to probe stellar interiors and their long-term evolution. Observations of lithium abundances in solar-type stars can be used to investigate rotation and the potential impact of external factors such as stellar and sub-stellar mass companions. + Effective temperatures of FGK stars

The most important observational parameter of a solar-type star is its effective temperature (roughly its surface temperature). It can be measured in many different ways, but probably the most accurate technique is the so-called InfraRed Flux Method (IRFM). Indeed, angular diameters derived using IRFM effective temperatures agree almost perfectly with direct interferometric measurements.
